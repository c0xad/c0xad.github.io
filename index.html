Working Paper

Tariff-Jumping Mergers and Acquisitions: Evidence from Asian Markets

Eren Gündemir
Working Paper, 2024

This paper examines the relationship between tariff rates and cross-border mergers and acquisitions (M&As) in five major Asian economies from 2008 to 2022. Using a comprehensive panel dataset combining tariff data with M&A transactions, we test the tariff-jumping hypothesis, which suggests that firms use foreign direct investment to bypass trade barriers.

Our findings provide strong support for this hypothesis in the context of cross-border acquisitions, with higher tariff rates and tariff increases associated with significantly increased M&A activity in the following years. We document substantial heterogeneity in this relationship, with the tariff-jumping effect being stronger for horizontal M&As compared to vertical M&As, weaker in intermediate goods sectors relative to final goods sectors, and exhibiting an inverted U-shaped pattern over time.

We also find that greater tariff uncertainty, as measured by tariff dispersion and binding overhang, is associated with increased acquisition activity. These results enhance our understanding of how trade policy shapes firms' international expansion strategies and highlight the importance of considering acquisitions as a distinct entry mode in response to trade barriers.

Working Paper

The Impact of the 1948 Monetary Reforms on the German Economy: A Quantitative Analysis

Eren Gündemir
Working Paper, 2024

This paper provides a comprehensive quantitative analysis of the 1948 currency reform in West Germany, examining its effects on inflation, output, and monetary dynamics. Using modern time-series techniques and structural break analysis, we find that the reform led to an immediate 25.4% reduction in price volatility and established a new monetary regime with sustained stability.

While experiencing initial economic contraction, the economy showed significant recovery within 12 months. Our results provide important insights for modern monetary reforms in post-crisis economies. The analysis employs state-of-the-art econometric methods including structural break tests, regime switching models, and impulse response functions to isolate the causal effects of the monetary reform.

The findings contribute to the literature on monetary policy effectiveness and provide empirical evidence for the theoretical predictions regarding currency reforms in hyperinflationary environments.
